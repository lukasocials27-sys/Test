1.1 — Heredity & Environment
Nature (heredity)Genetic/predisposed traits influencing physical, behavioral, and mental characteristics.
Nurture (environment)External factors such as family, education, and culture that shape development.
Evolutionary perspectiveNatural selection affects behavior to increase survival and reproductive success. Eugenics is a harmful misapplication of this idea.
Research methodsTwin studies, family studies, and adoption studies are used to disentangle genetic vs. environmental effects on behavior.
1.2 — Nervous System
CNSBrain + spinal cord; interacts with all processes in the body.
PNSPeripheral nervous system; relays messages between CNS and body. Includes autonomic and somatic.
Autonomic NSGoverns involuntary processes. Subdivided into sympathetic (fight/flight/freeze) and parasympathetic (rest/digest).
Somatic NSGoverns voluntary muscle movement and control.
1.3 — Neuron & Neural Firing
Neurons vs. Glial cellsNeurons transmit information. Glial cells provide structure, insulation, support, and waste transport.
Reflex arcSensory → interneuron → motor neurons in the spinal cord; bypasses the brain for fast responses.
All-or-none principleA neuron either fires completely or not at all — once threshold is crossed.
Action potential stepsResting potential → threshold reached → depolarization → refractory period → reuptake (neurotransmitters reabsorbed).
AgonistsDrugs that mimic neurotransmitters and encourage neural firing.
AntagonistsDrugs that block receptors and discourage neural firing.
Reuptake inhibitorsBlock reabsorption of neurotransmitters back into the presynaptic neuron (e.g., SSRIs block serotonin reuptake).
Disorders from disruptionMultiple sclerosis (myelin damage slows signals), myasthenia gravis (acetylcholine receptor blockage causing muscle weakness).
Neurotransmitters — testable list only
DopamineReward, movement, motivation. Implicated in schizophrenia (excess) and Parkinson's (deficit).
SerotoninMood, sleep, appetite. Low levels linked to depression.
NorepinephrineAlertness, arousal, fight-or-flight response.
GlutamateMajor excitatory neurotransmitter; central to learning and memory.
GABAMajor inhibitory neurotransmitter; calms neural activity. Targeted by alcohol and anti-anxiety drugs.
EndorphinsNatural pain relief and euphoria; released during exercise.
Substance PTransmits pain signals; blocked by opioids.
AcetylcholineMuscle movement and memory. Low levels linked to Alzheimer's disease.
Hormones — testable list only
Adrenaline (epinephrine)Stress and excitement response; increases heart rate and energy.
LeptinSignals satiety (fullness) to the hypothalamus.
GhrelinSignals hunger; stimulates appetite.
MelatoninRegulates circadian rhythm and sleep-wake cycle.
OxytocinBonding, trust, and social connection; released during physical contact.
Psychoactive drug categories
StimulantsIncrease neural activity (caffeine, cocaine). Tolerance and addiction possible.
DepressantsDecrease neural activity (alcohol). Slow CNS function.
HallucinogensDistort perception and cognition (marijuana, LSD, psilocybin).
OpioidsPain relief; act on endorphin receptors (heroin, morphine, oxycodone).
1.4 — The Brain
Brain stem / medullaControls basic life functions: breathing, heart rate, blood pressure.
Reticular activating systemAlertness, voluntary movement, some learning and emotion.
CerebellumCoordination, balance, and procedural/motor learning.
ThalamusSensory relay station (all senses except smell route through here).
HypothalamusHomeostasis (hunger, thirst, temperature, sex drive); regulates pituitary gland.
HippocampusMemory formation; converts short-term to long-term memory.
AmygdalaEmotional processing, especially fear and aggression.
Occipital lobesVisual processing (rear of brain).
Temporal lobesAuditory and language processing (sides of brain).
Parietal lobesSomatosensory cortex (touch) and spatial processing (back crown).
Frontal lobesLanguage, higher-order thinking, executive function; motor cortex at rear.
Broca's areaSpeech production (left hemisphere). Damage → Broca's aphasia (can understand but not produce speech).
Wernicke's areaSpeech comprehension (left hemisphere). Damage → Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless speech).
Corpus callosumConnects and allows communication between hemispheres.
Brain plasticityBrain's ability to rewire and compensate for damage by forming new connections.
Research toolsEEG (electrical patterns), fMRI (blood flow/activity), lesioning, case studies.
Key experiment
Split-brain research
Roger Sperry & Michael Gazzaniga — Nobel Prize 1981
Severing the corpus callosum (used to treat severe epilepsy) revealed hemispheric specialization. Images shown only to the left visual field (processed by right hemisphere) could be picked out but not named — demonstrating left hemisphere language dominance and contralateral brain organization.
1.5 — Sleep
Circadian rhythm~24 hour biological clock. Melatonin regulates it. Jet lag and shift work disrupt it.
NREM Stages 1–3Progressively deeper sleep. Hypnagogic sensations occur entering Stage 1. Duration decreases across the night.
REM sleepParadoxical sleep: brain waves similar to waking but body is paralyzed. Dreaming occurs. Frequency increases as night progresses. REM rebound follows deprivation.
Dream theoriesActivation-synthesis: random brain signals, cortex constructs narrative. Consolidation theory: REM sleep consolidates and organizes memories.
Why we sleepMemory consolidation and restoration of depleted biological resources.
Sleep disorders — testable only
InsomniaNarcolepsyREM sleep behavior disorderSleep apneaSomnambulism (sleepwalking)
1.6 — Sensation
SensationDetecting environmental stimuli and transducing them into neurochemical signals for the brain.
Absolute thresholdThe minimum stimulus intensity detectable 50% of the time.
Just-noticeable difference (JND)The minimum detectable change in stimulus intensity.
Weber's LawThe change needed to detect a difference is a constant proportion of the original stimulus strength.
Sensory adaptationDiminished sensitivity to a constant, unchanging stimulus over time.
SynesthesiaOne sense is experienced through another (e.g., hearing colors).
RodsPeriphery of retina; detect shapes and movement, not color; active in low light; enable dark adaptation.
ConesFovea of retina; detect color and fine detail; blue/green/red types (trichromatic theory).
Trichromatic theoryThree cone types (RGB) combine to produce all color perception.
Opponent-process theoryGanglion cells work in opponent pairs (R/G, B/Y, Bk/W); explains afterimages and color vision deficiency.
Blind spotWhere the optic nerve exits the eye; no photoreceptors here; brain fills in the gap.
AccommodationLens changes shape to focus; impairment leads to nearsightedness or farsightedness.
Pitch theoriesPlace theory (high frequency), frequency theory (low frequency), volley theory (mid-range).
Sound localizationIdentifying where sounds originate based on timing/intensity differences between ears.
Hearing loss typesConduction deafness (middle ear damage); sensorineural deafness (cochlea or auditory nerve).
OlfactionOnly sense that bypasses the thalamus; goes directly to the limbic system (emotion + memory).
GustationSix basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami, oleogustus. Supertasters have more taste receptors.
Gate control theoryPain signals can be blocked or modulated in the spinal cord (e.g., rubbing an injury helps).
Phantom limb sensationSensation felt in an amputated limb; demonstrates that pain is processed in the brain.
Vestibular senseBalance; detected primarily by semicircular canals in the inner ear.
KinesthesisSense of body position and movement without looking.
ProsopagnosiaFace blindness; caused by occipital lobe damage.
BlindsightResponding to visual stimuli without conscious awareness; caused by visual cortex damage.

2.1 — Perception
Bottom-up processingDriven by raw sensory data — perception built from individual features upward.
Top-down processingDriven by prior knowledge, expectations, and context — the brain fills in gaps.
SchemasMental frameworks built from experience that filter and shape perception.
Perceptual setA predisposition to perceive things in a certain way based on prior expectations.
Gestalt principlesClosure (fill gaps), figure/ground (object vs. background), proximity (nearness = grouping), similarity.
Cocktail party effectSelective attention — you hear your name mentioned even in a noisy environment.
Change blindnessFailure to notice changes to the environment when attention is elsewhere (inattentional blindness).
Binocular depth cuesRetinal disparity (slight difference between each eye's image) + convergence (eyes turning inward for close objects).
Monocular depth cuesRelative clarity, relative size, texture gradient, linear perspective, interposition — work with one eye.
Perceptual constanciesObjects appear stable despite changing retinal images (size, shape, color constancy).
2.2 — Thinking, Problem-Solving & Decision-Making
Concepts & prototypesConcepts are mental categories; prototypes are the best/most typical example of a category.
AssimilationFitting new information into an existing schema without changing it.
AccommodationChanging an existing schema to incorporate new information that doesn't fit.
AlgorithmsMethodical problem-solving: try all possible solutions. Guaranteed correct but slow.
HeuristicsMental shortcuts for quick judgments. Fast but error-prone.
Representativeness heuristicJudge probability by how well something matches a stereotype/prototype.
Availability heuristicJudge likelihood by how easily an example comes to mind.
Mental setTendency to use solutions that worked before, even when a new approach would work better.
PrimingPrior exposure to a stimulus influences later perception or decision-making.
FramingHow a choice is presented influences the decision made (e.g., "90% survival" vs. "10% mortality").
Gambler's fallacyBelieving past random events affect future random probabilities (they don't).
Sunk-cost fallacyContinuing an endeavor because of past investment rather than future value.
Executive functionsPrefrontal cortex processes: planning, organizing, goal-directed behavior, critical thinking.
Functional fixednessInability to see new uses for familiar objects; blocks creative problem-solving.
Divergent vs. convergentDivergent thinking = many novel solutions (creative). Convergent = single best answer.
2.3–2.5 — Memory: Models, Encoding & Storage
Explicit memoryConsciously recalled: episodic (personal events) + semantic (facts and general knowledge).
Implicit memoryNot easily verbalized: procedural memory (skills and habits like riding a bike).
Prospective memoryRemembering to do something in the future ("remember to call at 5 pm").
Long-term potentiationRepeated synaptic firing strengthens neural connections — the biological basis of memory formation.
Multi-store modelAtkinson-Shiffrin: sensory memory (iconic/echoic) → short-term memory → long-term memory.
Working memory modelCentral executive + phonological loop (verbal) + visuospatial sketchpad (visual) — Baddeley & Hitch.
Levels of processingStructural (shallowest) → phonemic → semantic (deepest, most memorable — Craik & Lockhart).
ChunkingGrouping information into meaningful units to expand working memory capacity.
Method of lociMnemonic device: place information in imagined spatial locations (memory palace).
Spacing effectDistributed practice over time beats massed practice (cramming) for long-term retention.
Serial position effectPrimacy effect (remember beginning of list) + recency effect (remember end). Middle items worst recalled.
Maintenance rehearsalSimple repetition; keeps info in short-term memory temporarily.
Elaborative rehearsalConnecting new information to existing meaning; promotes long-term retention.
2.6–2.7 — Retrieval & Forgetting
Recall vs. recognitionRecall = retrieve without cues (essay). Recognition = identify from options (multiple choice).
Context-dependent memoryBetter recall in the same physical environment as encoding.
Mood-congruent memoryBetter recall when in the same emotional mood as during encoding.
State-dependent memoryBetter recall in the same physiological state as encoding.
Testing effectSelf-testing improves long-term memory more than re-reading alone.
Forgetting curveRapid forgetting immediately after learning, then leveling off over time (Ebbinghaus).
Proactive interferenceOld information interferes with learning/recalling new information.
Retroactive interferenceNew information interferes with recalling old information.
Tip-of-the-tongueKnowing you know something but being unable to retrieve it — inadequate retrieval cues.
RepressionPsychodynamic: unconscious blocking of memories to protect the ego from distress.
Misinformation effectMisleading post-event information distorts memory of the original event (Elizabeth Loftus).
Source amnesiaRemembering information but forgetting where or when you learned it.
Constructive memoryMemories are reconstructed each time they're recalled, not played back like recordings.
Retrograde amnesiaLoss of memories formed before brain damage.
Anterograde amnesiaInability to form new long-term memories after brain damage (hippocampus).
Key experiment
Misinformation effect — car crash studies
Elizabeth Loftus
Participants watched a car accident film then answered questions. Those asked "How fast were the cars going when they smashed?" recalled higher speeds and falsely remembered broken glass compared to those asked with "hit." Demonstrated that memory is reconstructive, not reproductive — and revealed dangers of leading questions in eyewitness testimony.
2.8 — Intelligence & Achievement
General intelligence (g)Spearman's single underlying factor accounting for performance across mental tasks.
Multiple intelligencesGardner: 8+ separate intelligences (linguistic, logical-math, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalist).
IQ (historical formula)Mental age ÷ chronological age × 100. Modern IQ uses standardized scores.
StandardizationTest administered under consistent procedures with norms from a representative sample.
ValidityTest measures what it claims to measure. Construct validity + predictive validity.
ReliabilityTest yields consistent results across administrations. Test-retest + split-half reliability.
Flynn EffectIQ scores have risen across generations worldwide; attributed to better nutrition, healthcare, and education.
Stereotype threatAwareness of a negative stereotype impairs test performance in the stereotyped group.
Stereotype liftAwareness of a positive stereotype boosts performance.
Fixed mindsetIntelligence is innate and cannot change (Carol Dweck).
Growth mindsetIntelligence can be developed through effort and learning.
Achievement testsMeasure what someone currently knows.
Aptitude testsPredict how someone will perform in the future.

3.1 — Developmental Psychology Themes & Methods
Cross-sectional researchCompare different age groups at one point in time. Faster but cohort effects can confound results.
Longitudinal researchFollow the same individuals over time. More accurate but slow, costly, and subject to attrition.
Stability vs. changeDo personality traits persist, or do people substantially change through life?
Continuous vs. discontinuousDoes development occur gradually (continuous) or in distinct stages (discontinuous)?
3.2 — Physical Development
TeratogensEnvironmental agents (drugs, alcohol, illness, toxins) that harm prenatal development.
Rooting reflexInfant turns toward touch on the cheek — survival reflex for feeding.
Visual cliffApparatus with a glass floor appearing as a drop-off. Even crawling infants avoid it, demonstrating early depth perception.
Critical/sensitive periodsWindows during development when experience has its greatest impact (e.g., language acquisition).
ImprintingNon-human animals attach to the first moving object seen after birth (Lorenz and geese).
PubertyAdolescent growth spurt; development of primary sex characteristics (reproductive organs) and secondary (body hair, voice). Menarche (first period), spermarche (first ejaculation).
3.4 — Cognitive Development (Piaget & Vygotsky)
Piaget's 4 stages
Sensorimotor (0–2 yrs)Learn via senses and action. Object permanence develops (objects exist when out of sight).
Preoperational (2–7 yrs)Language/symbols emerge; pretend play; egocentrism; animism. Lacks conservation, reversibility. Theory of mind begins to develop.
Concrete operational (7–11)Logical thinking about concrete objects; can conserve. Still struggles with abstract/hypothetical thinking.
Formal operational (12+)Abstract and hypothetical reasoning. Not all individuals fully reach this stage, per Piaget.
VygotskyChildren are social learners. Zone of proximal development (ZPD) = tasks doable with help but not alone. Scaffolding = support provided by a more knowledgeable other.
Crystallized intelligenceAccumulated knowledge and vocabulary; stable through adulthood and into old age.
Fluid intelligenceAbility to reason quickly with novel problems; tends to decline with age.
3.5 — Language Development
Language featuresShared arbitrary symbols; governed by phonemes, morphemes, semantics, grammar, and syntax; generative (infinite novel sentences possible).
Developmental stagesCooing → babbling → one-word (holophrastic) stage → telegraphic speech ("want milk") → full sentences.
OvergeneralizationApplying grammar rules too broadly — e.g., "I goed to the store" — shows rule learning, not rote memorization.
3.6 — Social-Emotional Development
Ecological systems (Bronfenbrenner)Microsystem → mesosystem → exosystem → macrosystem → chronosystem. Each layer influences development.
Parenting stylesAuthoritarian (strict, low warmth), authoritative (firm + warm = best outcomes), permissive (warm, low structure).
Attachment typesSecure; insecure-avoidant; insecure-anxious; disorganized. Influenced by temperament and caregiver responsiveness.
Separation anxietyHeightened fear or distress when separated from a primary caregiver; peaks around 8–12 months.
Adolescent egocentrismImaginary audience (feeling constantly watched/judged) + personal fable (feeling unique and invincible).
Social clockCulturally defined timeline for expected life events (marriage, career, children); varies across cultures.
ACEsAdverse childhood experiences have lasting effects on attachment and relationships throughout life.
Identity development (Marcia)Achievement (explored + committed), diffusion (neither), foreclosure (committed without exploring), moratorium (exploring, not yet committed).
Harlow's contact comfort experiments
Harry Harlow
Infant rhesus monkeys chose a cloth "mother" over a wire "mother" that provided milk — proving that comfort and contact matter more than food in attachment. Contradicted the behaviorist view that feeding creates attachment, and established that social bonds serve emotional needs.
Erikson's 8 psychosocial stages
Trust vs. mistrustInfancy
Autonomy vs. shame & doubtToddlerhood
Initiative vs. guiltEarly childhood
Industry vs. inferiorityMiddle childhood
Identity vs. role confusionAdolescence
Intimacy vs. isolationYoung adulthood
Generativity vs. stagnationMiddle adulthood
Integrity vs. despairLate adulthood
3.7 — Classical Conditioning
DefinitionLearning by associating one stimulus with another to elicit a response. Pavlov's foundational dogs experiment.
UCSUnconditioned stimulus — naturally and automatically triggers a response (food).
UCRUnconditioned response — natural, unlearned reaction (salivation to food).
CSConditioned stimulus — previously neutral; now triggers response after pairing (bell).
CRConditioned response — learned response to the CS (salivation to bell alone).
AcquisitionLearning the CS-UCS association. CS must consistently precede the UCS.
ExtinctionCS presented without UCS repeatedly → CR weakens and disappears.
Spontaneous recoveryAn extinguished CR reappears after a rest period when CS is presented again.
Stimulus generalizationResponding to stimuli similar to the original CS.
Stimulus discriminationResponding only to the specific CS and not to similar stimuli.
Higher-order conditioningAn established CS is used as a UCS to condition a new CS.
CounterconditioningReplacing a conditioned fear response with a relaxation response. Basis of exposure therapy.
Taste aversionOne-trial learning of food-illness association; demonstrates biological preparedness — species are wired to learn certain pairings faster.
HabituationDiminished response to a repeated, unchanging stimulus (not technically conditioning, but related).
Little Albert experiment
John B. Watson & Rosalie Rayner (1920)
Conditioned 9-month-old "Albert" to fear a white rat by pairing it with a loud, startling noise (UCS). Fear generalized to other white furry objects. Demonstrated classical conditioning of emotional responses. Considered deeply unethical by modern standards — no extinction trials were conducted, leaving the child conditioned to fear.
3.8 — Operant Conditioning
Law of Effect (Thorndike)Behaviors followed by satisfying consequences are more likely to be repeated; those followed by discomfort are less likely.
Positive reinforcementAdd something pleasant → increases behavior (praise after good work).
Negative reinforcementRemove something unpleasant → increases behavior (taking aspirin to stop a headache). NOT punishment.
Positive punishmentAdd something unpleasant → decreases behavior (speeding ticket).
Negative punishmentRemove something pleasant → decreases behavior (grounding = removing phone/freedom).
Primary reinforcersNaturally rewarding (food, water, sex).
Secondary reinforcersLearned value (money, grades, praise).
ShapingReinforcing successive approximations of a target behavior until the full behavior is achieved.
Instinctive driftConditioned behavior eventually reverts toward instinctual behavior patterns (Brelands).
Learned helplessnessAfter repeated uncontrollable aversive events, an organism stops trying even when control becomes possible (Seligman).
Superstitious behaviorAccidental reinforcement of an unrelated behavior (wearing a "lucky" shirt that happened to precede success).
Reinforcement schedules
ContinuousReinforce every correct response. Fastest acquisition, fastest extinction when stopped.
Fixed-ratio (FR)After a set number of responses. High steady rate; brief pause after reward (piecework pay).
Variable-ratio (VR)After unpredictable number of responses. Highest and most persistent rate; most resistant to extinction (slot machines, gambling).
Fixed-interval (FI)After a set time period. Scalloped pattern — response rate increases as reward time approaches (weekly paychecks).
Variable-interval (VI)After unpredictable time periods. Steady, persistent response rate (checking email, social media).
3.9 — Social, Cognitive & Neurological Learning
Social learning theory (Bandura)Learning occurs through observation; vicarious conditioning — you don't need personal experience with consequences.
Insight learning (Köhler)Sudden "aha" solution without prior trial-and-error. Köhler's chimps stacked boxes to reach bananas.
Latent learning (Tolman)Learning that occurs without reinforcement but is demonstrated later when motivation exists. Shown through cognitive maps (mental spatial layouts).
Bobo doll experiment
Albert Bandura (1961)
Children who observed an adult aggressively hit an inflatable "Bobo" doll later imitated those exact behaviors — without any direct reinforcement themselves. Established observational/social learning theory: behavior can be learned vicariously through watching models, especially similar ones. Challenged strict behaviorism.

4.1 — Attribution Theory & Person Perception
Dispositional attributionExplaining behavior by internal qualities — personality, character, intelligence.
Situational attributionExplaining behavior by external circumstances and context.
Fundamental attribution errorOverestimating dispositional factors and underestimating situational factors when explaining others' behavior. Very common in Western cultures.
Actor/observer biasWe explain our own behavior situationally but explain others' behavior dispositionally.
Self-serving biasAttribute successes to self (disposition) but failures to situation.
Explanatory styleOptimistic (events are temporary, specific, external) vs. pessimistic (permanent, global, internal).
Locus of controlInternal (I control my outcomes) vs. external (fate/luck/others control outcomes — Rotter).
Mere exposure effectRepeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking of it.
Self-fulfilling prophecyExpectations cause behaviors that confirm those very expectations (e.g., Pygmalion effect).
Social comparisonWe evaluate ourselves by comparing to others. Upward comparison (to those better off) or downward (to those worse off).
Relative deprivationFeeling deprived relative to others, even when objectively you may have enough.
4.2 — Attitude Formation & Change
StereotypeA generalized belief about a group. Reduces cognitive load but produces biased judgments and prejudice.
Implicit attitudesAttitudes held unconsciously; measured by IAT. Can conflict with explicit (stated) attitudes.
Just-world phenomenonBelief that people get what they deserve; tends to blame victims for their misfortune.
In-group biasTendency to favor members of one's own group over outsiders.
Out-group homogeneity biasPerceiving out-group members as all the same while viewing the in-group as diverse.
EthnocentrismJudging other cultures by the standards and values of one's own culture.
Belief perseveranceClinging to a belief even when evidence contradicts it; maintained through confirmation bias.
Cognitive dissonance (Festinger)Mental discomfort from conflict between actions and attitudes; motivates change in one of them to restore consistency.
4.3 — Psychology of Social Situations
Normative social influenceConform to be liked or accepted by the group.
Informational social influenceConform because others seem to have better information about the correct answer.
Conformity (Asch)Adjusting behavior or judgment to match the group norm, even against one's own perception.
Obedience (Milgram)Compliance with authority figure commands. 65% delivered maximum shocks in original study. Situational factors dominate.
Elaboration likelihood modelCentral route (thoughtful analysis of facts) vs. peripheral route (superficial cues like attractiveness or reputation).
Foot-in-the-doorAgree to a small request first → more likely to comply with larger request later.
Door-in-the-faceMake a huge request first (which is refused) → then make the smaller real request (which is granted).
Halo effectA positive overall impression based on one trait (e.g., attractiveness) influences judgments of unrelated traits.
Group polarizationGroup discussion intensifies the group's initial leaning — moderate views become more extreme.
GroupthinkDesire for harmony suppresses dissent; leads to poor collective decision-making.
Diffusion of responsibilityEach individual feels less personal responsibility when more people are present.
Bystander effectAs group size increases, probability of any individual helping decreases (Kitty Genovese case, Darley & Latané).
Social loafingIndividual effort decreases when working in a group — harder to identify individual contributions.
DeindividuationLoss of self-awareness and restraint in group situations; leads to impulsive or antisocial behavior.
Social facilitationPresence of others improves performance on simple or well-practiced tasks; impairs performance on complex/new tasks.
False consensus effectOverestimating the extent to which others share our beliefs and behaviors.
Superordinate goalsShared goals requiring intergroup cooperation; reduce conflict and stereotyping (Robbers Cave study).
AltruismSelfless helping. Social reciprocity norm (help others to receive help) and social responsibility norm explain prosocial behavior.
I/O psychologyIndustrial-organizational: applies psychology to workplace productivity, management, relationships, and burnout.
Milgram obedience study
Stanley Milgram (1963)
Participants believed they were administering electric shocks to a "learner" (confederate) for wrong answers. 65% continued to the maximum 450V level when an authority figure instructed them to. Demonstrated the extraordinary situational power of authority over individual moral judgment. Major ethical concerns: deception, psychological distress, unclear right to withdraw.
Asch conformity study
Solomon Asch (1951)
Participants matched line lengths. When 7 confederates unanimously gave the wrong answer, ~75% of real participants conformed at least once — even though the correct answer was obvious. Conformity increased with group size and was sharply reduced if even one confederate agreed with the participant. Demonstrated normative social influence.
Stanford Prison Experiment
Philip Zimbardo (1971)
Students randomly assigned as "guards" or "prisoners." Guards became abusive; prisoners became passive and distressed. Experiment terminated after 6 days. Showed how social roles and deindividuation lead to extreme behavior. Widely criticized for ethical violations: lack of clear consent, failure to stop harm, and later critiques questioned methodology and data reliability.
4.4 — Psychodynamic & Humanistic Personality
Psychodynamic theory (Freud)Unconscious drives shape personality. Id (pleasure principle), ego (reality principle), superego (moral principle).
Defense mechanismsDenial, displacement (redirecting emotion), projection (attributing to others), rationalization, reaction formation (opposite), regression, repression (blocking), sublimation (channeling into socially acceptable acts).
Projective testsAmbiguous stimuli designed to reveal unconscious thoughts — Rorschach inkblot test, Thematic Apperception Test (TAT).
Humanistic theory (Rogers)People have a self-actualizing tendency. Unconditional positive regard — being valued without conditions — fosters healthy personality development.
4.5 — Social-Cognitive & Trait Theories
Reciprocal determinism (Bandura)Behavior, cognition (thoughts/beliefs), and environment mutually influence each other in a continuous loop.
Self-efficacyBelief in one's own ability to successfully perform specific tasks. Strongly predicts behavior and achievement.
Self-esteemOverall global sense of self-worth.
Big Five (OCEAN)Openness to experience, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (emotional stability). Measured by personality inventories using factor analysis.
4.6 — Motivation
Drive-reduction theoryBiological needs create drives; motivated behavior reduces those drives and restores homeostasis.
Arousal theory + Yerkes-DodsonPeople seek optimal arousal. Performance peaks at moderate arousal (inverted U curve); too little or too much impairs performance.
Intrinsic motivationDriven by internal interest, enjoyment, or personal satisfaction.
Extrinsic motivationDriven by external rewards (money, grades, praise). Overjustification effect: excessive external reward can undermine intrinsic motivation.
Lewin's motivational conflictsApproach-approach (two good options), approach-avoidance (one option has pros and cons), avoidance-avoidance (two bad options).
Sensation-seeking theoryNeed for novel/varied experiences as motivation. Types: thrill-seeking, experience-seeking, disinhibition, boredom susceptibility.
Hunger regulationGhrelin (hunger signal) and leptin (satiety signal), both regulated by hypothalamus via pituitary gland. External cues (food presence, time) also influence eating.
4.7 — Emotion
James-Lange theoryPhysiological arousal occurs first → then we label it as an emotion. "We are afraid because we run."
Cannon-Bard theoryPhysiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously and independently.
Two-factor theory (Schachter-Singer)Arousal + cognitive label = emotion. The same arousal can become different emotions depending on how it's labeled.
Facial-feedback hypothesisFacial expressions can influence emotional experience — smiling may increase positive feelings. Research results are mixed.
Broaden-and-build theoryPositive emotions broaden awareness and build psychological resources. Negative emotions narrow thinking and action options.
Universal emotions (Ekman)Anger, disgust, sadness, happiness, surprise, and fear may be cross-culturally consistent. Display rules vary by culture and gender.

5.1 — Health Psychology & Stress
EustressMotivating, positive stress that enhances performance and well-being.
DistressDebilitating stress that harms health. Linked to hypertension, headaches, and immune suppression.
General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)Alarm stage (fight-flight-freeze response) → Resistance stage (confronting and coping) → Exhaustion stage (resources depleted; most vulnerable to illness here).
Tend-and-befriend theorySome people respond to stress by tending to their own needs and/or seeking social connection. More commonly observed in women.
Problem-focused copingAddress the stressor directly by working to change or solve the problem.
Emotion-focused copingManage the emotional reaction to stress through meditation, deep breathing, or medication.
5.2 — Positive Psychology
Positive psychologyScientific study of factors that promote well-being, resilience, and flourishing — not just the absence of disorder.
GratitudePracticing gratitude increases subjective well-being and life satisfaction.
Signature strengthsUsing one's character strengths linked to higher happiness. Six virtue categories: wisdom, courage, humanity, justice, temperance, transcendence.
Posttraumatic growthPositive psychological change — including improved relationships, new possibilities, and stronger sense of meaning — that can follow trauma.
5.3 — Explaining & Classifying Disorders
Criteria for disorderDysfunction (impairs daily functioning) + distress (causes personal suffering) + deviation from social norms.
DSMDiagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (APA). Standard diagnostic tool in the US; updated regularly.
ICDInternational Classification of Diseases (WHO). Global equivalent; used internationally.
Biopsychosocial modelPsychological disorders arise from a combination of biological, psychological, and sociocultural factors.
Diathesis-stress modelGenetic vulnerability (diathesis) + stressful life events (stress) = disorder. Neither alone is sufficient.
Eclectic approachMost clinicians draw on multiple theoretical perspectives when explaining and treating disorders.
Theoretical perspectives on causes
BehavioralMaladaptive learned associations between stimuli and responses.
PsychodynamicUnconscious conflicts and unresolved childhood experiences.
HumanisticLack of social support; inability to self-actualize or meet one's potential.
CognitiveMaladaptive thoughts, beliefs, and attitudes distort perception and behavior.
EvolutionaryBehaviors/processes that reduce survival or reproductive likelihood.
SocioculturalMaladaptive social and cultural dynamics, stigma, discrimination.
BiologicalPhysiological dysfunction, genetic predisposition, or neurochemical imbalances.
5.4 — Psychological Disorders (testable categories only)
Neurodevelopmental
ADHD and Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). Onset during development; behaviors inappropriate for age/maturity. Causes: environmental, physiological, or genetic.
Schizophrenic spectrum
Positive symptoms: delusions (false beliefs — persecution, grandeur), hallucinations (false perceptions), disorganized speech (word salad), catatonic excitement. Negative symptoms: flat affect, catatonic stupor. Dopamine hypothesis; genetic + prenatal viral exposure.
Depressive disorders
Major depressive disorder (MDD) and persistent depressive disorder. Persistent sad/empty mood + cognitive/physical changes impairing function. Biological, genetic, social, cultural, behavioral, and cognitive causes.
Bipolar disorders
Bipolar I: full manic episodes + depression. Bipolar II: hypomania + depression. Cycling between states. Biological and genetic causes; treated with lithium.
Anxiety disorders
Specific phobia; agoraphobia; panic disorder (ataque de nervios = culture-bound variant); social anxiety disorder (taijin kyofusho = Japanese culture-bound variant); generalized anxiety disorder (GAD — nonspecific chronic worry).
OCD & related
OCD: obsessions (intrusive thoughts) + compulsions (repetitive behaviors intended to reduce obsession distress). Hoarding disorder. Learned associations, maladaptive thinking, biological/genetic causes.
Dissociative disorders
Dissociative amnesia (with or without fugue) and dissociative identity disorder (DID). Dissociation from consciousness, memory, or identity. Causes: trauma and severe stress.
Trauma & stressor-related
PTSD: hypervigilance, flashbacks, insomnia, emotional detachment, hostility following a traumatic event. Cause: trauma/extreme stress exposure.
Eating disorders
Anorexia nervosa (severe restriction of food) and bulimia nervosa (binge-purge cycles). Biological, genetic, social, cultural, cognitive causes.
Personality disorders
Cluster A (odd/eccentric): paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal. Cluster B (dramatic/erratic): antisocial, histrionic, narcissistic, borderline. Cluster C (anxious/fearful): avoidant, dependent, OC personality disorder.
5.5 — Treatment of Psychological Disorders
Evidence-based practiceTreatments supported by meta-analytic research. Psychotherapies are generally effective; therapists should use best available evidence.
Therapeutic allianceThe working relationship between therapist and client; one of the strongest predictors of therapy success.
Cultural humilityTherapist's ongoing, self-reflective awareness of their own biases and their client's cultural background.
DeinstitutionalizationShift from hospitals and asylums to outpatient/community-based treatment; driven by development of psychotropic medications.
Psychotherapy approaches
Psychodynamic therapyFree association and dream interpretation to uncover unconscious conflicts.
Cognitive therapyCognitive restructuring; targets the cognitive triad (negative thoughts about self, world, future — Beck).
Behavior therapy / ABAExposure therapy, systematic desensitization (graduated fear exposure + relaxation), aversion therapy, token economies, biofeedback.
CBTCombines cognitive + behavioral approaches. Includes dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) and rational-emotive behavior therapy (REBT).
Person-centered therapyHumanistic; active listening and unconditional positive regard (Rogers).
Group therapyShared experience; social support; cost-effective vs. individual therapy.
HypnosisEffective for pain management and anxiety. NOT supported for accurate memory retrieval or age regression.
Biomedical treatments
AntidepressantsTarget serotonin/norepinephrine (SSRIs, SNRIs). Used for depression, anxiety, OCD.
Antianxiety drugsBenzodiazepines target GABA; reduce anxiety quickly but risk dependence.
LithiumMood stabilizer for bipolar disorder; requires regular blood monitoring.
AntipsychoticsTarget dopamine for schizophrenia. Side effect: tardive dyskinesia (involuntary movement disorder).
ECTElectroconvulsive therapy; effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression.
TMSTranscranial magnetic stimulation; non-invasive brain stimulation for depression.
LobotomyHistorical prefrontal psychosurgery; rarely if ever performed today.
APA ethical principles for clinicians
Nonmaleficence — do no harm Fidelity — keep commitments Integrity — honest practice Respect for rights and dignity
